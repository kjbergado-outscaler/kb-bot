Weight blankets may be an effective and safe intervention to foster calm, thus providing an innovative, non-pharmacological approach and a complementary tool to improve sleep quality. However, study evidence here mostly relates to patient groups. The size and weight of the blanket should be individualized to the person using it so that it is neither too heavy nor too light.



Scientific evidence on the impact of weighted blankets


Studies conducted so far primarily focused on patients suffering from depression, anxiety, ADHS (attention deficit hyperactivity disorder), autism spectrum disorder, for example


According to those studies, a weighted blanket can indeed help to feel calm and sleep better. Here’s a brief summary on selected studies:


Study 1 (by Ekholm, B., Spulber, S., & Adler, M. (2020)):

  • 120 patients (major depression, bipolar disorder, generalized anxiety, ADHS) slept with either a weighted metal chain blanket or a light plastic blanket for 4 weeks, sleep and daytime activity was monitored by wrist actigraphy

  • After 4 weeks, the weighted blanket showed a significant advantage over the light blanket in terms of patients' insomnia (assessed by the Insomnia Severity Index) with a large effect size (Cohen's d 1.90)

  • Patients who slept with a weighted blanket experienced:

    • better sleep maintenance,

    • higher daytime activity levels,

    • reduced daytime fatigue symptoms

    • depression and anxiety were also reduced in this group

  • Side effects did not occur

  • With continued use of the weighted blanket, the effects persisted during a 12-month follow-up

  • Patients who switched from a light blanket to a weighted blanket later in the study also benefited and showed similar improvements in sleep and symptom reduction



Study 2 (by Ackerley, Rochelle & Olausson, Håkan & Badre, Gaby. (2015)):

  • 31 insomnia patients, not suffering from any other mental or physical disorder

  • Following a baseline (pre-test) with their ordinary sleep blanket, study participants slept for 2 weeks with a weighted blanket; afterward, they returned the blanket and slept again for 1 week with their ordinary blanket (post-test)

  • Sleep was measured using continuous actigraphy (Actiwatch) and comprehensive polysomnography (PSG) recordings in the participants’ own home

  • Objective results of the study showed that the use of a weighted blanket compared to an ordinary sleep blanket resulted in:

    • an increase in sleep duration

    • a decrease in nocturnal movements

  • Subjective assessments of the study participants were also documented and showed:

    • that participants liked using the weighted blanket

    • found it easier to fall asleep

    • slept better

    • felt more rested in the morning



What’s the magic behind it?

What could be the underlying mechanisms for the positive effect of weighted blankets?


  • When we are stressed, our heart beats faster than usual

  • This is accompanied by an activation of the sympathetic nervous system (also responsible for the activation of the fight-or-flight system), and our body is on edge

  • To counteract this, (moderate) pressure may help, because: through pressure, the counterpart to the sympathetic nervous system, namely the parasympathetic nervous system, becomes activated, which results in a lowering of the heart rate

  • This creates a feeling of calm and relaxation

  • A weighted blanket may by this evoke sensations similar to the feeling of being hugged, caressed or held tightly

  • A study (by Reynolds, S., Lane, S. J., & Mullen, B. (2015)) confirmed the effect of pressure stimulation on the activity of the sympathetic/parasympathetic nervous system:

    • In a sample of 50 adults, it was shown that wearing a pressure vest (Vayu Vest) could reduce sympathetic arousal and simultaneously increase parasympathetic arousal after short periods of only 3 minutes

    • In addition, better cognitive performance in brainteasers was observed, which, according to the authors, maybe due to the lower arousal when wearing the pressure vest




What’s inside a weighted blanket?


  • Fills can vary according to various parameters (above all, quality, cost and comfort).

  • The most common fillers are steel beads, micro glass beads, sand, pebbles, and grains.

    • Beads of steel are a popular filler for weighted blankets because they are robust, machine washable, and hefty, requiring fewer units to produce the required weight and pressure. Steel beads' primary disadvantage is typically louder than other materials.

    • Glass microbeads are denser than other materials, which means less filler is needed, resulting in a thinner form. Micro glass beads are also machine washable.

    • Sand may be used as a filler material, but it's not particularly popular. Indeed, clumping may be a significant issue when it comes to washing a sand-filled weighted blanket. In reality, following machine washing, specific sand blankets may not return to their former shape.

    • The greatest problem with pebbles is that they are not always comfortable and their use can finally result in a less restorative sleep experience.

    • Weighted blankets, may also be filled with uncooked grains, pasta, and dry beans. Grains and beans are widely accessible and low-cost, making them an appealing material for certain producers. On the other hand, these materials degrade with time, and are so, they are not a particularly long-lasting choice.
