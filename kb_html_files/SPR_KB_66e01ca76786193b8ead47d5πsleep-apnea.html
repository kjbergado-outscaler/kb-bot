What is sleep apnea?


Sleep apnea is a disorder that involves the complete or the temporary stopping of breathing at intervals of at least 10 seconds during sleep. This leads to a decrease in oxygen content in the blood and sleep fragmentation.


It usually affects men aged between 40 and 70 years old. It also commonly occurs in elderly individuals and those suffering from obesity. In general, this sleep disorder can be divided into three categories:


  • Obstructive apnea: This involves real airway obstruction.
  • Central apnea: In this case, the malfunctioning of the respiratory activity is due to a temporary loss of the nervous stimulus on the way to the respiratory muscles.
  • Mixed apnea: This last type is simply a mix of the two previous types of apnea.

Symptoms and effects of sleep apnea


Usually, the most common symptom of sleep apnea is snoring. In fact, due to the dysfunction of the breath, this becomes stronger. For example, prolonged breathing pauses, groans, and puffs are observed in sleepers who suffer from sleep apnea.


The causes of sleep apnea can vary, but aggravating factors include the use of sedatives, hypothyroidism, nasal obstruction, or the supine position during sleep.


The effects of this disorder may include difficulty falling asleep at night, excessive tiredness during the day, frequent headaches, and concentration problems.


Moreover, it has also been identified as a risk factor for other clinical consequences such as systematic hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stroke.


Studies have also shown that men suffering from sleep apnea have significantly lower levels of the hormone testosterone than those of a matching comparison sample not suffering from sleep apnea.


Risk factors associated with sleep apnea that might increase vulnerability for the disorder. The risk factors are advancing age, male sex, obesity, family history, menopause, craniofacial abnormalities, heavy cigarette smoking, and alcohol use.



Medical treatment and advice


Although sleep apnea is recognized as a serious disease, it often stays undetected. Here, sleeping partners or familiars can help to report any abnormalities during the night (e.g. heavy snoring, breathing problems/pauses during sleep).


Early recognition helps appropriate and effective therapy, counteracting serious health consequences.


Treatment of milder forms of sleep apnea includes weight reduction, quitting smoking, maintenance of a regular sleep pattern, or treatment of nasal allergies to support better breathing at night.


If symptoms do not improve or in case of severe sleep apnea, various treatments/devices are possible: the most common and reliable method is CPAP (Continuous positive airway pressure), a machine you put on before going to bed which delivers air pressure through a mask to keep the upper airway passages open. Others are oral appliances designed to keep your throat open, or supplemental oxygen machines to counteract a shortage of oxygen during sleep. But these are only a few examples of a range of possible treatment methods.
