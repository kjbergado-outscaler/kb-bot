Why does light matter in general?


As per our Circadian Rhythm article, Light is the main synchronizer, also called key Zeitgeber, of our internal circadian clock which defines our ~24h sleep-wake cycle.


  • Light affects the production of melatonin, a sleep-inducing hormone that signals night to the body.
  • Melatonin production slowly increases at the onset of darkness and stays elevated for the course of an approximately twelve-hour period.
  • By this, melatonin helps maintain the body’s internal sleep-wake cycle which repeats roughly every 24 hours and is hence called a circadian rhythm (from Latin “circa diem”, for “approximately one day”, cf. card on Sleep Cycle or Circadian Rhythm).
  • Although melatonin does not participate in the generation of sleep itself, it increases the sleep pressure we feel (i.e. tiredness and need to sleep), and thus times when sleep is going to occur.
  • During the day, however, melatonin levels should be low as we do not want to feel tired. The suppression of melatonin by natural/artificial light is hence intended and called for during the day as it promotes attention, alertness, and mood.

What happens when we are exposed to light?


The exposure of light induces phase shifts of our clock resulting in either direction:

  • a delaying: biological rhythm shifts to a later clock time with light exposure in the evening, or
  • advancing: biological rhythm shifts to an earlier clock time with light exposure in the morning.

The use of light therapy to treat circadian rhythm sleep disorders has proven to be a potentially effective treatment.


In one study, the authors examined whether 30 min blue-light pulses are effective in shifting sleep with preservation of sleep integrity and performance in a home-setting light therapy study. The authors found that:

  • blue-light therapy was able to support a sleep phase by increasing a shift in the melatonin rhythm and improving sleep timing.

In another study, scientists used a sleep mask to provide a train of light pulses of 2-second duration every 30 seconds through closed eyelids. They found that:

  • blue light pulses given early in the sleep episode impacted the melatonin secretion and significantly delayed circadian phases in older adults thereby, adjusting circadian phase in a home setting.

Blue Light



Exposure to blue light, emitted by the screens of TV, tablets, computer, mobiles etc., can adversely impact sleeping behaviour and sleep continuity by inhibiting melatonin, a sleep-inducing hormone.


A study conducted at Harvard University revealed that blue light suppresses the production of melatonin for twice as long as does green light. Hence, although the exposure of blue light during the day will help us to stay alert by suppressing melatonin, exposure in the evening / at night can be detrimental to sleep quality.


Cajochen et al., also showed that evening expsoure to blue light lead to suppression of melatonin release more than green light, thereby, offsetting the carcidian rhythm (our internal sleep-wake pattern of approx. 24h, cf. card on Circadian Rhythm) and reducing sleepiness. Blue light exposure also elevated the core body temperature (however, a drop in core body temperature helps us to fall asleep and maintain sleep) and heart rate (which also lowers when we calm down and fall asleep).


A study showed that participants who read an e-book before bedtime took an average of nearly 10 min longer to fall asleep compared to individuals who read a printed book. Reading an e-book before bedtime also led to:

  • reduced melatonin release,
  • decreased sleepiness before bedtime,
  • significantly less REM sleep (stage of sleep where most dreaming occurs, cf. card on REM Sleep),
  • and increased levels of morning grogginess.

Artificial Light


What effect does artificial light have?


  • Exposure to artificial light emitted by recent technologies, such as LEDs, computer screens, mobile phones or televisions suppresses melatonin production, delaying its release in the body.
  • This creates a phase delay, which means that the peak of melatonin occurs later than the time it would otherwise, leading to restless sleep and late sleep timings.
  • Exposure to artificial lighting can hence delay the circadian clock resulting in late sleep timing and social jetlag (a discrepancy between biological and social timing), which may ultimately lead to detrimental effects on health, such as depression, cancer, cardiovascular diseases.
  • Persons, shift workers, for example, who are regularly exposed to unusual light-dark cycles, are therefore exposed to sleep disturbances and serious health risks.


What effect does natural light have?


  • In contrast, natural light induces the synthesis of melatonin.
  • Exposure to natural light aligns the circadian rhythm to the natural light-dark cycle of the environment, resulting in improved sleep patterns and earlier bedtimes.
  • Human bodies can adjust the production of melatonin according to changes in the light-dark cycle of the environment:
  • the circadian clock adapts to the seasonal variations, naturally shortening (sleeping shorter) during the short summer nights and lengthening (sleeping longer) to align with the long winter nights.
  • A camping weekend in natural light can reset the circadian timing and help alleviate social jetlag that contributes to Monday morning grogginess.
