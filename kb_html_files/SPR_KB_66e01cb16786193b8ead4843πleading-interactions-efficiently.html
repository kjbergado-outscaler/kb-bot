When we look at the customer service business and the metrics we have in place to measure our success, many of them will be related to time, mostly defining how efficiently we handle customers' inquiries.



But why is time such an important metric for customer service?




When you are contacting customer service, how much would you enjoy being transferred to different people to talk about one single issue?


Or, how likely would you recommend a service that is not able to provide a response at first, and you would have to call later to have visibility of your order status?


How about the experience of being put on hold, and waiting for 10 minutes in the line while you have no idea about what is happening on the other side of the line?


Time defines your experience with the service you receive.


But on the other hand, the question you should have to yourself when having a CS experience should be:


Was your contact handled fast and efficiently?

Did you get the answers you were looking for?

Were you able to learn where to find information and avoid a returning contact for it?

Was your time respected while interacting with the customer service?



Those are some rules we should take into consideration while handling customer interactions:




1. Educate the customer

whenever possible, educate the customer on any available channel we may have to avoid a returning call, for example, educate him to find proper answers in our Frequently Asked Questions, email or account, whenever the information is available. Empower our customers to self portal and have no reasons to invest the time calling us back.





2 . Antecipate procedures

If the customer will need to send us a picture, provide already the next step indications, right directions, email needed, or information up-front. Anticipate needs by respecting their time. This will directly impact their experience with us.






3. Do not postpone information

Do not avoid conflict or negative messages.

Transparency is part of our DNA and avoidinh pr this will may manage bad customer expectations and take more time to an honest and necessary outcome.





4. Do not escalate cases when the workflow is clear

Escalation usually has more negative impacts than positive ones. It shows a lack of decision and ownership of our frontline, open procedures and by the end, pushes decisions that usually are already in place.

Be careful with customer expectations and the time you take to provide an answer to the customer's inquiries.



5. Hold etiquette

Ensure the customer agrees to wait in the line while you put them on hold in a phone interaction, or be clear you will take some internal steps while live chatting with the customer. Always thank the customer when returning, showing appreciation for the time waiting.





6. Closure question

Always ensure there is no open question from the customer:
"Is there anything else I can help you with?"
Closure questions guide the customer through their needs and avoid a returning contact from the customer as you are already ready to support this customer with you.




7. Respect Closing time

Respect customer time until the end of the interaction, ensure the customer takes enough time to end up the call by not rushing it to the end and negatively impacting the customer's experience with us. The closure should always focus on a positive feeling, as the customer will be motivated to reply positively to a Csat survey that may come after it.
