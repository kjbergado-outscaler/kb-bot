Insomnia is the most common sleep disorder and refers to an inadequate ability to generate sleep, despite allowing oneself the adequate opportunity (!) to fall asleep. Meaning, patients provide enough time for rest (e.g. 7-9 hours at night) but cannot produce sufficient sleep quantity or quality.


Insomnia can be divided into specific sub-types, although they are not mutually exclusive, which means they might occur together or separately:


  • Onset Insomnia: Difficulty falling asleep
  • Maintenance Insomnia: difficulty staying asleep


To be diagnosed with insomnia it must occur at least three nights each week for more than three months (to separate insomnia from temporary sleep problems), lead to significant stress or daytime impairment, and stand independent from other mental disorders or medical conditions that could otherwise cause sleep disturbance (e.g. depression, Parkinson’s disease, diabetes).

Prevalence rates of insomnia are estimated at 10-30% of adults; however, rates are likely to be far more widespread with women affected twice as often as men.


The two most common triggers of insomnia are emotional concerns or worry, and emotional distress or anxiety.



On the biological level, emotional arousal leads to an overactive sympathetic nervous system, which controls the body’s fight-or-flight mechanism, resulting in increased heart rate, blood flow, metabolic rate, brain activity, and release of the stress-negotiating hormone cortisol. All of which makes it extremely difficult for our body to rest and fall asleep.


Treatment


The treatment for insomnia includes medication, psychotherapy, or behavioral therapy.

On the one hand, they only promote sleepiness for a short time, thereby delaying the time needed to fall asleep, but distorting the natural sleep pattern (more sleep in N2, less REM sleep, and deep sleep).


On the other hand, sleeping pills make people highly dependent on them while at the same time creating tolerance for the sleep-promoting effects, i.e. an ever-higher dose is necessary to achieve the same effects, and once patients stop taking the medication, they frequently suffer even more from insomnia than before, which is called rebound insomnia (the brain alters its balance of receptors as a reaction to the increased drug dose).


In addition, those taking the medications often suffer from fatigue and reduced alertness the next day, whereby chronic use of sleeping pills even leads to a reduction in general life expectancy.


For this reason, the use of sleeping pills should only be used infrequently and in a highly controlled manner!!!


Another and more promising approach to counteract insomnia is cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I). Here, patients learn a set of techniques to improve sleep habits, address anxieties, for example. CBT-I allows for patient-focused treatment strategies and responses to individual needs.
