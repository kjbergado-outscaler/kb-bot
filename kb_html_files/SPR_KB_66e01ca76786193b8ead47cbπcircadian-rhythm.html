Intro


The circadian rhythm, also known as our Inner clock, does not only manage when we sleep but actually all regular behavior, as eating for example.


The inner clock is determined by different factors as gym, hormones, temperature, and our behavior. However, the most important and influencing factor comes from light.


Through our eyes, Our brain is constantly receiving signals by receptors sensitive of blue eye - setting out our internal clock


Besides, other processes, substances created also raise the need of sleep.




What is a circadian rhythm?


Let’s start at the very basic: The term circadian comes from Latin and is composed of the terms circa (approximately) and Diem (day)

In chronobiology, circadian rhythms are defined as processes with a period length of about 24 hours, hence the circa + Diem.

These cycles are determined internally, we speak of an endogenous rhythm or an internal clock - in contrast to processes that are generated by external factors and therefore exogenous.


The ~24-h rhythm repeats itself continuously unless we influence it by external forces - but more about that in a minute.


Probably the best-known circadian rhythm is the sleep-wake rhythm, which causes us to switch repeatedly between sleep and wakefulness - yesterday, today, and tomorrow.


However, other physiological parameters also exhibit a rhythm with a ~24-hour period, e.g. our body temperature or the secretion of certain hormones fluctuate in a 24-hour rhythm.



Why do we need a circadian rhythm?

With the rotation of the earth, there are periodically recurring changes such as changes from brightness to darkness, warmer and colder temperatures, but also aspects such as availability of food or lurking threats.


Living organisms that adapt to such changes can optimize their ability to function and thus ensure their chances of survival.

This is why circadian rhythms are not only found in humans, but also in animals and plants (activation of photosynthesis).


The essential function of the circadian rhythm is to classify life processes in terms of time.


For example, the activation and deactivation of individual processes in the body enable us to sleep and regenerate at night but to be fully functional, alert, and active during the day.



How does the circadian rhythm (in humans) work on a neurobiological level?


Circadian rhythms throughout the body are linked to a central circuit, sometimes called the circadian pacemaker.


This pacemaker is located in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) - part of the hypothalamus - just behind the root of your nose.

The SCN is very sensitive to light, which is why light exposure is closely linked to the circadian rhythm.

In darkness, the SCN sends a corresponding signal to the pineal gland, which in turn produces, among others, the sleep-inducing hormone melatonin.








What happens when our circadian rhythm is out of tact?

If the circadian rhythm is disturbed, it has major influences on our ability to function properly.


Our hormone production would go haywire, blood pressure, body temperature or heart rate, etc, would no longer follow the sequence to which we have adapted over the years.


Not only can this pose serious health risks, but it would also significantly reduce our well-being and productivity.


If our internal clock no longer signals "night" to our body, it stays on high alert. Consequently, we have difficulty falling asleep and repeatedly wake up during the night or early in the morning.


This is accompanied by sleep fragmentation, lower sleep efficiency, and also poorer quality sleep.



What factors can influence our circadian rhythm?


Light is the main synchronizer, also called key Zeitgeber, of our internal circadian clock:

  • Light affects the production of melatonin, the sleep-inducing hormone that signals night to the body.
  • Melatonin production slowly increases at the onset of darkness and stays elevated for the course of an approximately twelve-hour period.
  • Although melatonin does not participate in the generation of sleep itself, it increases the sleep pressure we feel (i.e. tiredness and need to sleep), and thus times when sleep is going to occur.
  • During the day, however, melatonin levels should be low as we do not want to feel tired.
  • The suppression of melatonin by natural/artificial light is hence intended and called for during the day as it promotes attention, alertness, and mood.
  • For more information on the impact of light cf. our card on “Light & Circadian Rhythm” > “Artificial Light” / “Blue Light” / “Light Therapy”


Our social activities can have an impact, too:

  • Breakfast/dinner times, social events and parties in the evening, etc. can have an impact on our daily rhythm as they give a temporal structure to our everyday life.
  • The relationship between the circadian rhythm and food intake is also used for nutrition tips: this is referred to as the principle of chrononutrition or time-restricted-eating (TRE)
    • It is a form of intermittent fasting in which daily food intake is adapted to the circadian rhythm and limited to a period of 4-12 hours
    • It does not require calorie restriction, but a consistent daily eating window
    • A systematic meta-analysis of 19 studies from 2020 (Moon et al.) found positive effects of TRE on our bodies, including weight loss, reduction in fat mass whilst maintaining fat-free mass, blood pressure and cholesterol profile
  • Depending on its intensity, we even speak of social jetlag

Jetlag - A factor all too familiar to globetrotters and business travelers:

  • When traveling time zones, our circadian rhythm takes longer to adapt to the new time zone than the airplane on which we traveled.
  • Consequently, our internal clock is somewhat desynchronized with the new environment.
  • This may lead to daytime sleepiness, headaches, stomach problems, or difficulties falling asleep.
  • Jetlag is particularly hard to push when traveling eastward.

What are chronotypes and how are they related to circadian rhythms?

While all of us have circadian rhythms, this does not mean that they are all in sync between individuals.


While some people may sleep earlier, or reach the lowest point of body temperature earlier in the evening, others may find sleep only later in the night, etc.


These inter-individual differences in the circadian rhythms are expressed in different chronotypes.


Generally speaking, a distinction is made between the morning type (also known as the lark) and the evening type (known as the owl).


Morning types wake up earlier and reach their cognitive and energetic peak already early in the day. In return, they also become tired in the evening more quickly and reach their lowest core body temperature earlier in the night than evening types, for example.


Evening types take longer to wake up in the morning - this may be due to a later onset of cortisol secretion - but are still wide awake when others are already fast asleep.


But of course, the story is not quite as black and white! There are many subcategories and by now a number of questionnaires or tests are available to determine one's own personal chronotype.
