Sleep Paralysis


As explained in our Sleep Cycles article, during REM sleep our skeletal muscles are completely paralyzed: no tone in the voluntary muscles of the body (involuntary muscles which control automatic operations like breathing continue to operate!)


Normally, when we wake up and return to consciousness, the brain releases the body from paralysis immediately. In the case of narcolepsy (a sleeping disorder), it may occur that paralysis prolongs into the state of being awake.


This leads to an inability to move your body â€“ not even eyelids or mouth. Within some minutes, the paralysis should cease, and full-body control should be regained.

Paralysis, however, is not unique to narcolepsy but can also occur among healthy individuals.

Generally, paralysis does not pose any worrying risks, however, if it occurs regularly or is getting worse, you should see your doctor.


It has not yet been fully understood why paralysis develops but it has been linked to other (sleeping) disorders or irregularities, e.g.: insomnia, disrupted sleeping patterns due to shift work or jetlag, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), anxiety disorders, panic disorder.


A regular sleep rhythm, measures that promote a good night's sleep in general, and adherence to sufficient sleep duration help to prevent paralysis.
