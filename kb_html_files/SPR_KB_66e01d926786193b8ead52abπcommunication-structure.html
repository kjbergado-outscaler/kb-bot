Email Structure

1. Initial greeting

* The contact opening *

Guidelines:

  • This part of the email should be used to greet the customer and to thank them for reaching out to us. However, it is important to always adapt this to match the circumstances.
  • We shouldn’t be rigid here as there is a lot of room for personalization and creativity, but we must make sure that our tone matches the customer’s situation.


Notes:

  • Agents shouldn’t use the same “thank you” statement during an email back-and-forth with a customer. This sentence should always be different.

Examples:

  • “Thank you for letting us know how to proceed.” (When the customer gets back to us with a decision on a pending issue.)
  • “Thank you for sending us the information we had requested.” (When the customer has provided us with pictures or additional information as per our request.)
  • “Thank you for your patience.” (When the customer was waiting for us to follow-up on something.)

2. Issue acknowledgement

* Showing understanding and empathy *


Guidelines:

  • This part of the email is meant to acknowledge the situation the customer is experiencing.
  • If the situation is negative, this is where we can show empathy towards the customer. See: Empathy.
  • Additionally, this is also where we start showing the customer that we are here to help, and we will. Commiting to help will always put a positive spin on our support. Customers need to be made aware that whatever their problem is, we will make it right.
  • In specific circumstances, this section can also be used to offer an initial apology. Example: “We are very sorry to hear that you have not been able to adapt to your new Emma mattress, and we sincerely apologise for not living up to your expectations on this occasion.”

3. Resolution

* Providing/framing the resolution *


Guidelines:

  • This is probably the most important part of our emails. In the eye of the customer, if this part fails, everything else becomes almost irrelevant.
  • In this section we provide or request information, we explain what happened, clarify doubts, and we educate the customer on our procedures. Additionally, whenever applicable and possible, we should set expectations regarding deadlines.
  • Agents should be clear, use positive language and try to be as concise as possible.
  • Personalization is always required. Our email replies should always be tailored to the situation at hand.

Notes:

  • Agents should make sure to break down a reply that addresses multiple issues into multiple paragraphs. Customers will feel lost in a big wall of text.
  • All information provided should be relevant to the customer and the situation at hand.
  • If the query is unclear, agents should not be afraid to question the customer so that they can understand the actual issue before offering a solution. However, in certain situations, this may not be necessary. Ex: If a customer contacts us about our “100 night warranty” on product X, we do not need to ask for clarification. Instead, we can just inform the customer that this product comes with a 50/100/200 night trial period and 2/5/10-year warranty. If we can clearly solve something in the first contact, without leaving loose ends, we should try to do it.

4. Closing statement

* Availability and farewell *

Guidelines:

  • This part of the email should be used to wrap up the contact in a nice and positive way. Thanking the customer for contacting us, for their patience during a lengthy process or for their cooperation in situations where we asked for pictures, for example. Additionally, this is also the part of the email where we leave the door open for future contacts by showing our availability to assist further should the need arises.
  • If the situation calls for it, we can also use this part to show empathy again. However, this will always depend on the context.


Added value:

  • If the agent feels this would be a good fit for the case at hand, a positive closing statement like “Have a nice day/Have a great weekend” can be added at the end of the closing statement section. Once again, this sort of statement is always dependent on a favorable context.

5. Signature

* Structure and format *

Signature rules:

  • The signature should always start with a polite ending to the email. “Best/kind regards” are the most common options but, depending on the situation, this can be personalized by the agent. However, please always make sure that more creative expressions, like “Sleep happy”, are not used in situations where the customer is aggrieved and may take this the wrong way.
  • Then, below the valediction, should come the agent’s name. This should only include the first name. If there is more than one agent with that specific first name, then the agents should include the initial of their last name (followed by a period) as well in their signature (ex: “Paul D.”).
  • Finally, below the agent’s name, should come the Emma logo (reflecting the current branding) and the company information right below.
