What happens when we suffer from jetlag?



Jetlag is a temporary disruption in circadian rhythm when travelling time zones.


Our body works on an internal ~24-hour sleep-wake cycle (called circadian rhythm, cf. card on circadian rhythm) which is influenced by and can adapt to environmental cues, such as light.

Review our Circadian Rhythm article here for more information.


When travelling time zones, our circadian rhythm takes longer to adapt to the new time zone than the aeroplane on which we travelled. Consequently, our internal clock is somewhat desynchronised with the new environment.



This may lead to daytime sleepiness, headaches, stomach problems or difficulties falling asleep.


Jetlag is particularly hard to push when travelling eastward. This is since phase-shortening (east is ahead of time to west) is more difficult to adjust to than phase-lengthening (west is behind east).

It is harder for us to fall asleep earlier as usual than staying up a bit longer when travelling westwards, especially since our circadian rhythm is innately a bit longer than 24 hours (~24 hours and 15 minutes), so eastward travelling contradicts our internal clock even stronger, whilst westward travelling approaches our internal clock.


Rule of thumb: it takes one day per time zone crossed to adjust, i.e. travelling 2 time zones takes approximately 2 days to adjust.


Exposing yourself to sunlight supports re-synchronising your circadian rhythm faster and fighting jetlag!
