The physical elements that interfere the most with our sleep are light and temperature. We know how light and a particular frequency of light can generate excitatory effects, inhibiting melatonin production and making us feel more alert and less willing to sleep.


A legitimate question in this respect is: which element, between light and temperature, can influence our sleep routines the most?




It is generally believed that in pre-industrial societies, the sleep of our ancestors was exclusively linked to the alternation of day and night. For this reason, many believe that in tribes and communities that lived without electricity, sleep coincided with sunset and awakening with sunrise. And for many years, this belief was also shared by academics.


In 2015, however, a scientific research article entitled Natural Sleep and Its Seasonal Variations in Three Pre-industrial Societies was published in the leading scientific journal Current Biology.


This article shows the results of a study conducted on three indigenous populations and attempts to answer the following question: How did humans sleep before the modern era? The answer to this question may help us to clarify the importance of light and temperature for our sleep.


It has been argued that the invention of the electric light, followed by the development of television, the Internet, and related technologies, along with increased caffeine usage, has dramatically shortened sleep duration from ‘‘natural’’ levels and disrupted its evolved timing. Is it true?



The researchers argue that:

  1. Light may not be as crucial for sleep patter as previously believed.

  2. The difference in sleep duration between our industrialized society and the pre-industrial one is not that great.

  3. The indigenous people have fewer sleep concerns when compared to post-industrial societies.

  4. Temperature plays a significant role in defining our sleep patterns.



The experiment concerned three tribes: Hadza from Tanzania, Tsimane from Bolivia, and San from Namibia. In these societies, electricity and its associated lighting and entertainment distractions are absent, as are cooling and heating systems.


Notably, none of these groups began sleep near sunset, with sleep onset occurring, on average, 3.3 hr after twilight. This shows how the rest of ancient societies were not synchronized with the alternation between night and day. On the contrary, the sleep period consistently occurred during the nighttime period of falling environmental temperature. It terminated, with vasoconstriction, near the lowest point of daily ambient temperature.



For this reason, the researcher concluded that the daily cycle of temperature change, largely eliminated from modern sleep environments, can be a potent natural regulator of sleep.


Another element that was researched was insomnia. Since it is a complaint that does not closely correlate to sleep, researchers evaluated the incidence of this complaint. The tribal population does not have a name for insomnia in their language.




5% of the participants indicated they occasionally had sleep onset issues.




5% sometimes had sleep maintenance problems.



Less than 1/3 of these people reported that they experienced these issues frequently, i.e., more than once a year (1.5 percent and 2.5 percent of the total number of participants).


These values are significantly lower than the 10–30 percent chronic insomnia rate observed in industrial cultures.


For what concerns light:


Moreover, the three groups regularly had tiny fires.


The research also showed that waking occurred on average 1 hour before dawn; This fact supports the belief that sleep wasn't strongly connected to solar light.


For what concerns temperature:


Let’s now analyze the impact of temperature. The researchers discovered that the nocturnal sleep phase in the indigenous people was always beginning at declining ambient temperature.


Therefore, they accurately monitored ambient temperature coupled with finger temperature and stomach temperature at the sleeping places. The results demonstrate that sleep in both the winter and summer occurred during declining ambient temperature and that awakenings happened towards the lowest point of the daily temperature cycle.


Why is this very relevant for us?


We can learn from this article the importance that temperature plays on the regularity of our sleep.

Contrary to earlier belief, it is not only light that plays a central role in our biological rhythms. Temperature is an even more potent synchronizer than light itself. As proof of this, the study cited above shows that our sleeping and waking rhythms coincide precisely with the lowering and raising of atmospheric temperature.



For this reason, we can use this knowledge to reinforce the idea that the correct temperature is fundamental to the quality of our sleep.
