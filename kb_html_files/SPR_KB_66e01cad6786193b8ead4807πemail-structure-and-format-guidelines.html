Introduction and disclaimer


This article is meant to act as a guideline on what we expect an email communication from Emma should look like. However, it is not meant to be seen as a set of restraints, but rather a platform on which agents can build upon.


Like with a phone call, our main goal will always be to have our emails come as close as possible to an actual conversation between two human beings. Hence, why allowing agents to find their voice and be naturally empathic is so important. We want contacts to be fluid and personalized.


Emma is a very agile company, so the agents representing it should never be (or sound) rigid.


Note: Always make sure to verify if there are other email addresses on CC or on the "To" list outside of the validated customer email and remove any additional emails.

Contact history should also be verified, as the customer will receive all the history, make sure there are no crossed emails or information the customer should not be receiving


This article is only applicable for email ticket contacts.


Email contacts structure breakdown


The five major dividers in an email contact are:


1. Initial greeting

* The contact opening *



Guidelines:

  • This part of the email should be used to greet the customer and to thank them for reaching out to us. However, it is important to always adapt this to match the circumstances.

  • We shouldn’t be rigid here as there is a lot of room for personalization and creativity, but we must make sure that our tone matches the customer’s situation.


Notes:

  • Agents shouldn’t use the same “thank you” statement during an email back-and-forth with a customer. This sentence should always be different.

Examples:

  • “Thank you for letting us know how to proceed.” (When the customer gets back to us with a decision on a pending issue.)

  • “Thank you for sending us the information we had requested.” (When the customer has provided us with pictures or additional information as per our request.)

  • “Thank you for your patience.” (When the customer was waiting for us to follow-up on something.)

2. Issue acknowledgement

* Context and empathy *


Guidelines:

  • This part of the email is meant to acknowledge the situation the customer is experiencing. If this situation is negative, this is where we can show empathy towards the customer.

  • Additionally, this is also where we start showing the customer that we are here to help, and we will. Commiting to help will always put a positive spin on our support. Customers need to be made aware that whatever their problem is, we will make it right.

  • In specific circumstances, this section can also be used to offer an initial apology. Example: “We are very sorry to hear that you have not been able to adapt to your new Emma mattress, and we sincerely apologise for not living up to your expectations on this occasion.”


Notes:

  • Agents should be aware that this is not a rephrasing exercise, emails are not phone calls.

  • Agents should always write as if they are speaking for Emma, as such, they should always use the pronoun “We”.

3. Resolution

* Providing/framing the resolution *


Guidelines:

  • This is probably the most important part of our emails. In the eye of the customer, if this part fails, everything else becomes almost irrelevant.

  • In this section we provide or request information, we explain what happened, clarify doubts, and we educate the customer on our procedures. Additionally, whenever applicable and possible, we should set expectations regarding deadlines.

  • Agents should be clear, use positive language and try to be as concise as possible.

  • Personalization is always required. Our email replies should always be tailored to the situation at hand.

Notes:

  • Agents should make sure to break down a reply that addresses multiple issues into multiple paragraphs. Customers will feel lost in a big wall of text.

  • All information provided should be relevant to the customer and the situation at hand.

  • If the query is unclear, agents should not be afraid to question the customer so that they can understand the actual issue before offering a solution. However, in certain situations, this may not be necessary. Ex: If a customer contacts us about our “100 night warranty” on product X, we do not need to ask for clarification. Instead, we can just inform the customer that this product comes with a 50/100/200 night trial period and 2/5/10-year warranty. If we can clearly solve something in the first contact, without leaving loose ends, we should try to do it.

4. Closing statement

* Availability and farewell *

Guidelines:

  • This part of the email should be used to wrap up the contact in a nice and positive way. Thanking the customer for contacting us, for their patience during a lengthy process or for their cooperation in situations where we asked for pictures, for example. Additionally, this is also the part of the email where we leave the door open for future contacts by showing our availability to assist further should the need arises.

  • If the situation calls for it, we can also use this part to show empathy again. However, this will always depend on the context.


Added value:

  • If the agent feels this would be a good fit for the case at hand, a positive closing statement like “Have a nice day/Have a great weekend” can be added at the end of the closing statement section. Once again, this sort of statement is always dependent on a favourable context.

5. Signature

* Structure and format *

Signature rules:

  • The signature should always start with a polite ending to the email. “Best/kind regards” are the most common options but, depending on the situation, this can be personalized by the agent. However, please always make sure that more creative expressions, like “Sleep happy”, are not used in situations where the customer is aggrieved and may take this the wrong way.

  • Then, below the valediction, should come the agent’s name. This should only include the first name. If there is more than one agent with that specific first name, then the agents should include the initial of their last name (followed by a period) as well in their signature (ex: “Paul D.”).


Notes:

  • There should be no reference to the Contact Center partner in the signature.

  • For Trustpilot reviews: since we don't have the Emma logo available, agents should sign "Paul from your Emma team".

Spacing

* The problem with visual noise *


What is the issue?

  • Structuring our email properly, by itself, does not guarantee that our message will get through to our customers. In modern days, we must always be aware that our customers’ attention span is very short. Having an email reply where all the information is cluttered in a large wall of text will undoubtedly cause frustration on the customers’ side, as this will prevent quick and easy access to the information. This can lead to misunderstandings and customers acting against or instructions. In extreme cases, this can even prompt the customer to give up entirely in trying to decipher our reply and just contact us again for further clarification, which will prevent us from handling this contact efficiently and add unnecessary workload onto our teams.

  • As such, in order to be effective, we need to make sure that we are being clear and precise in the information we are providing in writing but also that we are presenting said information in a way that is as free as possible of visual noise.

How do we tackle this?

  • By spacing our email sections properly, we are effectively reducing visual noise and making sure our message has a bigger chance of being successfully received by its recipient.


Why do we leave two blank lines before the signature?

  • Like with a normal letter, the signature on an email should be further detached from the email body. This is more professional and reinforces the notion that we are fully committed to a high standard of service.

Engagement opportunities

* Making connections *


Opportunities:

  • Agents should always make sure to adapt to the customer. Although our approach should be informal and fluid, if we encounter a customer that doesn’t respond well to this, we should change our approach to suit that customer’s needs (Ex: A customer that wants to be addressed as “Mr. Smith”, instead of “John”). Research has shown that messages that fit with a customer’s attitudes and dispositional motives are processed more fluently and evaluated more positively than incongruent messages.


  • Always look one step ahead. Customer interactions unfold like a chess match. If we know a certain action on our end will cause a reaction on the customer’s side, agents should make sure to take that into account when planning their next steps. Providing context and explaining why things are how they are, will always go a long way. Transparency is key, if we want our customers to trust us.


  • If a customer is excited about a product or a resolution, we should try to match their energy in our responses. Using appropriate punctuation marks and choosing the right words will be fundamental throughout the contact.


  • If the customer is using emojis and the context is favourable, or the situation was resolved with a positive outcome for the customer, agents can, in moderation, use emojis as well. This will act as positive reinforcement for the interaction and will enhance the connection between customer and agent. When using emojis, if, by default, the platform does not allow for full, colourful emojis during text entry, type Windows logo key + . (period). The emoji keyboard will appear.

Things to avoid

* Communication quicksand *


Things to avoid:

  • Agents should always make sure to spell the customer’s name correctly. This is a huge red flag and will immediately convey a poor image of our service standards. Customers should always be treated with respect.


  • Avoid repetition. Starting most sentences using the same words (Ex: We... We... We...) or using the same expressions multiple times (Ex: Please note... Please note...) will just reinforce the negative bias, many customers have nowadays, that CS agents are just copy/pasting templates.


  • When something goes wrong, focus on the solution, not on who is to blame for an issue. We are one team, we should all be pulling in the same direction.


  • Do not use unnecessary branding. It is fine to refer to products using our brand name (Ex: “Emma One mattress”). However, we should not be overusing the brand name on written communication.


  • Negative reinforcement should be avoided at all costs. Acknowledging that something did not go according to plan is one thing, reinforcing the negative aspect of a customer’s experience with us is another altogether. Always try to focus positively on the solution and what we will now do for the customer.


  • Information should be consistent. Agents should not provide conflicting instructions or guidelines to a customer.


  • When asking for additional information from a customer, agents should always make sure they request everything they need at once. If we have to ask the customer for more information/pictures afterwards, this will just cause frustration and create the belief that we are artificially blocking their path towards a solution.
