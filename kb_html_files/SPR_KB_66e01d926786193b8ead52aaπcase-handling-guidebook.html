Introduction


This article contains general guidelines on how support agents in Americas (MX, CL, CO, US, CA) should be handling customer tickets.

Resolution

When can we consider that a ticket is resolved?


Agents should only place a ticket in Resolved if we have provided a complete resolution to the customer's issue and all their questions have been answered. For example:

  • WIMO tickets should only be considered as resolved once you have provided a delivery date & a tracking number to the customer or they have cancelled it and the refund has been confirmed. Of course, we should try to achieve the first outcome in all tickets.
  • Pre Sale tickets should only be considered as resolved once all customer questions have been answered.
  • Return request tickets should only be considered as resolved once the request has been completely deflected (using all deflection tools available) or the customer has returned the product and we have received confirmation from the refunds team that they have processed the refund. We should also share clear expectations on the process and times from the payment processing entity (e.g. Mercado Pago).
  • Product claims should only be considered as resolved if the customer has received a replacement or has returned the product and we have received confirmation from the refunds team that they have processed the refund.


If the customer's issues, concerns or questions have not been fully resolved we should not place the case in Resolved. Instead, depending on the situation we should follow either one of the following steps:


If we need additional information from the customer to achieve resolution, we should send an email to the customer and place the case inWaiting on Customer.


Or


If we continue working on the resolution/are waiting from other teams, we should change the case status to In progress. Additionally, we should update the customer on the ticket's progress by following the Incremental updates guidelines.

Offering survey on the last email we send.


On the last email we send, before we mark the case as resolved we should remind customer's of the survey that will be sent.

Incremental Updates

Keeping customers updated on the status of their ticket.

As facilitators of the customer experience, we have a responsibility to proactively reach out to customers and provide updates on the steps we are following to resolve their concerns. We should never wait for customers to request for updates, this increases customer effort significantly.


Our updates should always follow the below requirements:

  • Information on what is being done.
  • And, clear expectations on when the customer can expect an update from our end.
    • Example: "I have raised your request with the team and I am currently waiting for a response on their end. Please expect an update from me by Monday July 11th or sooner".
  • Ideally, updates should happen every 24 business hours.

Follow up

How soon should we reply to customer emails?

Whenever customer reply to us asking for updates, replying with information we requested or with additional questions, we should always send an response within the next 24 business hours.


Case Documentation

Sharing meaningful insights on what has happened in the ticket and our next steps.

Since tickets might be handled by different people, it is very important for agents to leave clear documentation that can help guide other people involved in the case. Case comments should always include the following:


GDPR: [Y/N]
Customer issue: [Brief summary of the customer's concern]
Case progress summary: [Brief recap of case history]
Next steps: [What is needed to resolve the case]



Customer Experience

Communicating in a way that shows advocacy and reduces the perception of effort.


Do's

  • Replace negative language and phrases (e.g., no, can't, won't) with positive language (e.g., can, will).
  • Focus on what you can do.
  • Modify your communication style to best match the customer's.
  • Take ownership of the issue
  • Demonstrate interest in the customer's issue; make the customer feel like you are on their side.
  • Be transparent.
  • Suggest options or creative solutions that benefit the customer. Offer alternatives.
  • Convey understanding and empathy with genuine language and tone.
  • Give the customer any information you have that may be helpful in processing their request in the future. For example:
    • If the customer is reaching about an item that is out of stock, tell them when it will be next available.


Don'ts

  • Place the blame on policies or other departments.
  • Make assumptions about customers or their needs.
  • Focus in what cannot be done.
  • Use accusatory language or blame the customer.
