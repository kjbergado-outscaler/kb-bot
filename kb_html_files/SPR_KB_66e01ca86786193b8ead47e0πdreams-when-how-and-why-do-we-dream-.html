When do we dream?


It may seem unnecessary (but it is not) to point out that we dream while we sleep. Sleep is a multifaceted event characterized (more or less homogeneously) by the lack of processing of external sensory stimuli. Such a condition is comparable to sensory deprivation: a situation characterized by the (voluntary) absence of sensory stimuli to which, very often, correspond very vivid images that can even lead to hallucinations.



Why & how do we dream?


The brain’s primary purpose is now thought to be consciousness. If that’s the case, consciousness is just the brain’s interpretation and integration of all the data it has access to at any given moment. In this sense, it is possible to claim that dreaming and hallucinations are variants of the same theme, based on the premise that the brain is active in all its states (wakefulness, REM sleep, and NREM sleep).



  • During normal wakefulness, the brain overlooks internally produced activity in favor of sensory input from the outside.

  • Dreaming happens during sleeping because the brain is focused on endogenously evoked activity.

  • The brain attends to external and endogenous activities concurrently under atypical circumstances, such as sleep deprivation, sensory deprivation, or medicine or drug intake, resulting in hallucinations or wakeful dreaming.

  • Numerous neurologic disorders and illnesses that are linked to hallucinations support this theory.


From what has been said above, the idea that dreaming includes a reversed sequence of perceptual experiences is derived.

  • Instead of bottom-up (i.e., from sensations to higher cognitive elaborations), top-down is used (i.e., higher cortical levels activating lower levels).

  • Motivational systems in the brain are usually activated in response to goal-oriented activities. However, neural activity shifts away from the perceptual regions and toward the motor areas. Therefore, the dreamer imagines rather than engages in motivated behaviors.

  • In addition, the reflecting system in the limbic brain is inactive, causing the dreamer to mistake the dream for reality. Indeed, damage to this region makes it difficult to tell the difference between dreams and reality when awake.



FUN FACT


Actually, the neuroscientific conclusions drown above are not so revolutionary. Indeed, Aristotle more than 2400 years ago addressed dreaming faculty as follow:


“If, then, the exercise of the faculty of sight is actual seeing, that of the auditory faculty, hearing, and, in general, that of the faculty of sense-perception, perceiving; and if there are some perceptions common to the senses, such as figure, magnitude, motion, etc., while there are others, as color, sound, taste, peculiar [each to its own sense]; and further, if all creatures, when the eyes are closed in sleep, are unable to see, and the analogous statement is true of the other senses so that manifestly we perceive nothing when asleep; we may conclude that it is not by sense-perception we perceive a dream.”
