How is our sleep structured?


The way sleep is structured is referred to as Sleep Architecture.


Sleep is composed of a series of so-called sleep cycles.

A sleep cycle is composed of 4 sleep stages, which are divided into non-REM (N1, N2, N3) and REM sleep (REM = Rapid Eye Movement)



These 4 stages make up one sleep cycle, which takes around 90 minutes to complete.


Each stage of sleep has its own unique purpose fulfilling a distinct physiological and psychological function and they occur in the same sequence.


Adults need 5 sleep cycles per night, which takes up to 7- 8 hours.

What happens at each stage of sleep?


N1

The phase that transitions from wakefulness to sleep.

Begins within minutes of lying down, but can take longer depending on sleep habits, disturbances, sleep disorders, and each person's physiology.


The duration can vary between 1 - 7 minutes.



Physiological changes
  • Eyes close
  • Muscles relax
  • Core body temperature decreases
  • Your pineal gland begins to release the sleep-inducing hormone: melatonin
  • Your brain waves shift from alpha waves of relaxation to theta waves of sleep
  • You may start dreaming during this stage




Curiosity facts:


Some people may lose their sense of time and place during this phase.

We may not even know we are still asleep.

Quite easy to be disturbed during this stage.

Hypnic jerks: sudden contractions of the muscle that can wake us up (sense of falling or ripping) - considered normal though they can be worsened by caffeine, stress, or some medications.

The difficulty of entering the n1 stage is a common symptom of Insomnia.



N2

Half of the four cycles of sleep are spent in this stage.

The body starts preparing for deep sleep.

Though the stage is similar to the n1 stage, there are some key changes that occur:



Physiological changes
  • Eye movement stops
  • Reduced awareness of the environment
  • Lower heart rate and body temperature
  • Your muscles will tense and relax
  • Your upper airway muscles relax
  • Sleep spindles start
  • K complexes

The Sleep Spindles are essential for forming memories, remembering dreams, and helping transfer short-term to long-term memories.



K Complexes

are brain waves generated in the cortex that last more than half-second, can be seen on an EGG (electroencephalogram).


K complexes help us process information, meaning memory consolidation.

Help also to synchronize the sleep stages. They're believed to help us sleep by preventing us from waking up to non-threatening external stimuli, however too much time in this phase isn't a good thing. It can indicate a sleep disorder like sleep apnea, restless leg syndrome, or insomnia.

N3

Essential phase to body recovery and is the most restorative of the sleep stages.

Help with the growth and repair of bones, muscles, and tissues. It is essential for the immune system.

It starts usually 40 minutes after sleep.



Physiological changes
  • Your body releases hormones that help with appetite control
  • Blood flow to the brain decreases
  • While blood flow to your muscles increases which gives them oxygen and restorative nutrients
  • Your blood pressure and heart rate decreases even further
  • Memories continue to be consolidating
  • Breathing becomes slower
  • Sleep spindle activity decreases




It is the last non-rem stage and is commonly referred to as Slow Wave Sleep. This is a deep stage of sleep and it's difficult to be woken up during this time. If you woke up during this stage you may feel groggy and the mental performance will be slower for about 30 minutes due to the reduced cerebral blood flow during this stage.






REM

Last stage of the sleep cycle


During REM, the pons turns off signals to neurons in the spine, preventing us from acting out our vivid dreams.

  • Emotion processing and consolidation
  • heal emotional wounds
  • problem-solving skills
  • creativity



Physiological changes
  • Paralysis of the muscles
  • Rapid and random eye movements
  • Fluctuation in breathing
  • Circulation and body temperature unique to REM sleep
  • Vivid dreams
  • Mind is active
  • Brain waves are similar to those when awake




Failure to achieve REM sleep can lead to:

  • Fatigue and cognitive impairment
  • Reduced immunity
  • Impaired hormone production
  • Changes in metabolism
  • High risk of neurological disorders like dementia


An inefficient sleep cycle can actually lead to the same consequences of sleep deprivation including:

  • Reduced concentration or hand-eye coordination
  • Daytime sleepiness
  • Irritability






Sleep should always be sufficient and efficient.
