There is no "one size fits it all" answer to the question of how many hours of sleep we need, as the amount of sleep you need depends on a variety of factors such as age, health, lifestyle, etc.


But according to the National Sleep Foundation, the general recommendation is:

  • 14-17 hours for new-borns
  • 12-15 hours for infants
  • 11-14 hours for toddlers
  • 10-13 hours for pre-schoolers
  • 9-11 hours for school-aged children
  • 8-10 for teenagers
  • 7-9 hours for young adults and adults
  • 7-8 hours for older adults



Curiosity about teenagers sleep habits:

Teenagers may find evening hours especially attractive to socialize or relax and prefer to go to bed later. To still have their amount of sleep needed to feel refreshed they are hence also likely to sleep later in the morning than younger children. This also corresponds to the fact that our internal clocks (circadian rhythm) shift during puberty.

Sleep Scientists urge that all this be taken into account when it comes to everyday school life, as many teenagers were regularly deprived of important hours of sleep in the morning and consequently showed impaired mental performance.




"Am I getting enough sleep?"


If you are wondering whether you are getting enough sleep, here are three questions that might help you:

  • Do you have trouble getting up in the morning? / Would you have slept past your alarm clock?
  • Do you have trouble focusing during the day? / Can you function optimally without caffeine?
  • Do you regularly doze off during the day?





Deep Sleep


Deep sleep, also called slow-wave sleep because of its characteristic brain waves, is crucial to feeling refreshed the next morning.


Deep sleep is considered to be the most restorative sleep phase and is associated with vital functions, among such: production of growth hormones, strengthening of the immune system, repair and regrow of body tissues, and memory consolidation.


Deep sleep occurs in longer periods during the first third of the night with adults spending about 10-20% of total sleep time in deep sleep. However, the assumption that deep sleep alone determines how long we should sleep is wrong! Alongside deep sleep, the remaining sleep stages also bear significant functions.

What happens when you are sleep-deprived?


Not getting enough sleep leads to sleep debt, which is essentially the difference between the amount of sleep we get and the amount we need.


Studies have shown that people who sleep five hours or less on weekdays, but extra long on weekends, still pay a significant cost.


Among them, for example, is an increased weight gain due to: excessive caloric intake in the evening (the longer we stay awake, the more hormones are released that boost our hunger and appetite), reduced energy expenditure, and changes in the body's use of insulin; impaired cognitive and physical performance; increased sensitivity and irritability; and many more.


A study conducted by the AAA Foundation in Washington, DC, in 2016 quantified a driver’s relative risk of being involved in a car crash relative to their sleep deprivation.

They found that drowsy driving leads to significantly higher crash rates: up to 11.5 times the crash rate when sleeping less than 4 hours compared to drivers who had slept for at least 7 hours.


Therefore, it is crucial to get the recommended daily amount of sleep and practice sleep hygiene regularly – for your own good and for your fellow men!

What happens when you oversleep?


While we know that too little sleep can lead to serious and significant impairments, we cannot deduce a linear relationship between sleep and health/performance, according to the idea of “more is more”.


Rather, there seems to be an inverted U-shaped relationship: Neither too little nor too much sleep is advisable – but the golden mean.


Just like over-eating, over-drinking, over-breathing is detrimental to our health, over-sleeping is most likely to come with drawbacks too! It is best to remember that too much of anything can be bad.


From an evolutionary perspective, sleeping at night is considered adaptive to protect us from accidents or predators in the dark and to preserve energy reserves. During the day, however, our bodies are equipped to be active, to forage, and reproduce. An extended period of sleep would here be maladaptive!


Oversleeping is often associated with physical or mental disorders, such as sleep apnea, depression, or side effects of medications.

Effects that may be linked to over-sleeping include diabetes, obesity, back pain, depression, heart disease, for example.
