Introduction - How does the customer brain work


Only a fraction of our decisions is really well thought out. Over 90% of decisions are made on gut instinct. However, these decisions are also made in the brain. More precisely, in the lymbic system. In addition to our vital needs, hunger and thirst, five independent systems determine our actions. Besides the sexual system, we have a stimulus system, dominance system, balance system and a harmony system. 50% of the expression of these systems is genetically predisposed. The other 50% is formed through experience, knowledge and education.

In addition to the development in the brain, some hormones also play a role. In this article we focus on the effect of the hormone's cortisol, dopamine and testosterone and their impact on the customer and the salespeople.


There is a good reason why decisions are often taken without much thought. Our brain saves resources in this way. As strange as it sounds, our brain has a mechanism that prevents thinking. Our brain uses shortcuts to save energy. A little math's problem to illustrate this phenomenon. Don't think too long and intuitively give the answer. 


A ping pong racket and a ball cost €1.10 together. The bat is €1 more expensive than the ball. What do the products cost individually?


If you now spontaneously say that the racket costs 1€ and the ball 10 cents, then you are wrong.

Here, the price difference would only be 90 cents. The actual price is 1.05 for the bat and 5 cents for the ball. Here the price difference is 1€.


Why these shortcuts? Our brain makes up about 2% of our body mass. However, during strong thought processes it needs up to 20% of the energy. Without this technique, our brain would not be able to act quickly. It therefore ensures our survival..Many sales techniques exploit this effect.


Accordingly, our brain collects survival strategies in the course of its life. This has an interesting effect. The older we get and the more strategies we have accumulated, the harder it is for us to deviate from these processes. Our brain wants to fall back on the tried and tested and shies away from change. Our balance system becomes the guiding system for decisions.



Lymbic System


The four main systems sit opposite each other like adversaries. Someone who is driven by the stimulus system, who seeks change and is guided by innovation, rarely falls back on the tried and tested.


Someone who has egoistically based buying motives and seeks products that reflect their own status, because the dominance system has the upper hand, cannot be convinced by arguments aimed at emotional closeness.


The important thing is that there is no such thing as a person who is only Dominant or only Balance based. Rather, diverse profiles emerge. One important thing, however, is that one of the systems is always the strongest. Possible characteristics can be seen in the following diagrams.



Stimulus system


People who are driven by the stimulus system are also perceived as creative or curios people. They look for the new, the unusual. A portion of enthusiasm is also part of the sales pitch here.


Convince the customer with enthusiasm. You can be a little more excited during the conversation, because you don't have that much time to show the customer all the unlimited possibilities with your product.

Emotional closeness and trust play the central role here. The Harmony Seeker also sometimes lets the chips fall where they may. In spite of everything, great sensitivity is required. The Harmony System and the Balance System are very similar. The hormone cortisol plays the decisive role here.

Dominance

Status, determination and professional behavior are the keys to a dominant person's heart. These people do not want to be bored with rambling explanations. Precise arguments, clear sentences and also a clear direction in which to steer the conversation are important here. Dominant people are considered performers. The arguments should be tailored in such a way that the personal advantage from the product is clearly evident. This customer wants to be treated like a VIP.

How to identify each customer


This means that in addition to the actual customer demand, the demand analysis must also find out how the argument is presented to the customer. Small indications and subordinate clauses of the customer can point to the characteristics of the various systems. Some examples:


"What features does the product have?" The customer wants to know everything and be enthusiastic about the product. The customer is looking for an individual solution and is open to new things. Stimulus arguments are convincing here.


"What is the density?" The customer tries to shimmy along numbers and looks for indicators to compare. The customer is concerned about safety and does not want to make a wrong decision. Balance arguments have a high chance of success.


"I work in nursing and have back pain due to the heavy physical work!" A clear indication that the clustomer is altruistically motivated. A person who puts others first. Focus on Harmony arguments.


"I've looked at Tempur" A sentence that clearly shows "I have no problem affording Premium and I'll show it." Status-oriented argumentation is recommended for dominant persons. 


The key to using this analysis technique successfully is quite simple. Ask open questions and listen carefully.


How hormones change our behavior




As our brain changes over the years, the concentration of various hormones also changes. The hormones cortisol, dopamine and testosterone play the main role in purchasing decisions.


Dopamine is the curiosity hormone, which provides optimism, but also triggers action and activity in the brain.

Testosterone, which is often described as the "masculinity hormone", is responsible for self-confidence. The higher the testosterone level, the less a customer shies away from confrontation.

Cortisol is the stress and anxiety hormone. The higher the cortisol level, the more a client is concerned about safety and reliability. The customer prefers to buy what he knows.



This has an influence on the type of sales talk. While young people are open to new things and can probably be convinced with stimulus arguments, older customers are more likely to be in the balance system. Almost 65% of all customers aremainly driven by the balance system.


A distinction between genders also makes sense. The probability of meeting a dominant male is significantly higher. The reason for this is the higher average testosterone level in men.


Especially at a younger age, there is also a higher probability of finding women in the Harmony and Balance systems. The reason for this is on the one hand the lower testosterone level, and on the other hand the harmony system is strengthened as soon as children are involved.


This changes also the approach to customers. In women, the average concentration of the bonding hormone oestrogen and the "cuddle hormone" oxytocin is significantly more pronounced. Furthermore, the empathy centre in the female brain is larger than in the male brain. The harmony system is on average up to 20-30% stronger in women.


In men, the dominance system is more pronounced in the same proportion.


Important here: On average!

Just as there are men with particularly high sensitivity and great empathy, there are also very assertive but also aggressive women.


The stronger harmony system has an impact on perception. Women react more strongly than men on all sensory channels. Details are more strongly perceived and included. In addition, women take more time and often shy away from the risks associated with a purchase. One possibility to react here is to provide the goods with additional attributes.


Example: Instead of: The mattress fits your body perfectly.

Better: "The cuddly soft mattress adapts to your body shape."



Due to the stronger dominance system, men expect a status gesture from the salesperson. Men want to be taken seriously and also want to feel this. Due to the stronger dominance system, men are twice as interested in technology and computers.


The stronger dominance system also has an impact on decision-making. Men often only recognize rough and simple structures. Arguments must therefore be clear and simply structured. Since men can absorb less detail, they are often bored by lengthy details.


Pleasure and frustration


The hormones are like medals. Each has two sides. Besides rewarding the customer's brain, they are also responsible for punishment. The following diagram provides an overview.



Our reward system knows only one direction: more. It gets used to success. The best-known example is the bar of chocolate. After rewarding ourselves with a piece, we then reward ourselves until the end of the bar. Problematic. The brain quickly gets used to discounts and at some point, takes them for granted.



The punishment and avoidance system has its peculiarities. It hates losses. Our brain values losses about twice as much as gains. This has been proven by the Nobel Prize winner in economics, Daniel Kahnemann. This effect can be used in reverse sales psychology. So, before you give a discount, it is all the more interesting for the customer's brain to give something in addition.
